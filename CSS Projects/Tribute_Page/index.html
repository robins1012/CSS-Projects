Srinivasa Ramanujan

December 22, 1887-April 26, 1920

Early Life

Srinivasa Ramanujan was born on December 22, 1887, in Erode, Tamil Nadu, India. He grew up in Kumbakonam, where his father worked as a clerk and his mother was a housewife and a singer at a local temple. Ramanujan showed an early talent for mathematics, excelling in the subject during his school years.

Education and Early Career

Despite his brilliance in mathematics, Ramanujan struggled with other subjects and eventually dropped out of formal education. He continued to study mathematics independently, developing his own theories and solutions to complex problems.

Mathematical Contributions

Ramanujan’s work is renowned for its originality and depth. Some of his key contributions include:

  1. Infinite Series and Continued Fractions: Ramanujan developed new formulas and theorems in theseareas, some of which were groundbreaking at the time.
  2. Partition Function: He made significant contributions to the partition of numbers, which involves expressing a number as the sum of other numbers.
  3. Ramanujan Prime: He introduced the concept of Ramanujan primes, a special type of prime number.
  4. Hardy-Ramanujan Number (1729): This number is famously known for its unique properties, discovered during a conversation between Ramanujan and G.H. Hardy.

Collaboration with G.H. Hardy

In 1913, Ramanujan sent a letter to G.H. Hardy, a prominent British mathematician, detailing some of his findings. Recognizing Ramanujan's extraordinary talent, Hardy invited him to Cambridge University. Ramanujan traveled to England in 1914 and worked closely with Hardy, producing several important papers and expanding the boundaries of mathematical knowledge.

Personal Struggles and Health Issues

During his time in England, Ramanujan faced significant health challenges exacerbated by the cold climate and dietary restrictions. Despite these difficulties, his work remained highly productive. He returned to India in 1919 due to his deteriorating health.

Legacy and Honors

Srinivasa Ramanujan passed away on April 26, 1920, at the age of 32. His work has had a lasting impact on mathematics, influencing various fields such as number theory, infinite series, and continued fractions. Several honors and institutions bear his name, such as the Ramanujan Institute for Advanced Study in Mathematics and the Ramanujan Prize for young mathematicians from developing countries.

Notable Publications

  1. "Notebooks": Filled with thousands of theorems and mathematical concepts, many of which were later validated by mathematicians.
  2. "Collected Papers of Srinivasa Ramanujan": A compilation of his published work.

Quotes

  • "An equation for me has no meaning unless it expresses a thought of God."

Commemoration

Ramanujan’s birthday, December 22, is celebrated as National Mathematics Day in India. His life and work have been the subject of several books and films, including the biography "The Man Who Knew Infinity" by Robert Kanigel, which was adapted into a movie starring Dev Patel as Ramanujan.

Conclusion

Srinivasa Ramanujan's incredible journey from a self-taught mathematician in India to a globally recognized genius is an inspiring tale of talent, determination, and the power of pure mathematical thought. His legacy continues to inspire and challenge mathematicians around the world.
